The Via Francigena

It was the year 990 a.C., when, during his travel back to Rome, the Archbishop Sigeric noted the 79 "subamansiones de Roma usque ad mare", that is to say the stages that divided his solemn stride towards the native Canterbury. This ancient Via that connects Canterbury to Rome and to the harbours of Puglia was re-discovered by contemporary wayfarers that walk along a wanderful and surprising path. From 2001 the European Association of the Vie Francigene supports the development and the promotion of an itinerary, that, crossing Europe, retraces the history of an important part of ou continent. A thousand years of history through England, France, Switzerland and Italy can be found at the core of the European project. A "Via of cultures", as it was defined by Jacques Le Goff, along which we build the Europe of people.
